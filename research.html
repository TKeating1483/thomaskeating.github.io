Research Projects

Managing Eternity? A Study of Accountability in the Deep Time Management of Final Repositories for Nuclear Waste

(Swedish Research Council Research Project Grant, 2025–2027; Total value: 4,872,000 kr)

I am principal investigator on a Swedish Research Council funded project entitled “Managing Eternity? A study of accountability in the deep time management of Final Repositories for Nuclear Waste”, conducted in collaboration with Professor Steve Woolgar. The project examines how experts in Sweden and Finland are, in practice, managing the long-term stewardship of geological repositories for spent nuclear fuel.

Final repositories for nuclear waste are designed to remain secure for up to 100,000 years, presenting profound challenges for contemporary institutions tasked with ensuring accountability across extremely long temporal horizons. While repository systems rely on highly technical engineering and geological expertise, their governance also requires the development of new organisational practices capable of transferring responsibility, knowledge, and oversight across generations.

Through empirical research with scientists, engineers, regulators, and institutional actors involved in repository planning, the project investigates how accountability is conceptualised and enacted in the present when the relevant timescales far exceed those of existing political, legal, and organisational structures. The research contributes to debates in science and technology studies, environmental governance, and social theory concerning long-term responsibility, institutional durability, and the politics of “deep time” infrastructures.

Nuclear Diplomacy? Strengthening Civil Society Involvement with Final Repositories for Nuclear Waste

(Mistra Research Project Grant, 2025–2027; Total value: 4,910,625 kr)

I am also principal investigator on a Mistra-funded research project entitled “Nuclear Diplomacy? Strengthening Civil Society Involvement with Final Repositories for Nuclear Waste”, conducted with co-investigators Professor Anna Storm and Dr Jenny Sjöholm.

This project examines how civil society organisations participate in and contribute to expert planning processes surrounding Sweden’s final repository for spent nuclear fuel. Geological repositories are designed as long-term infrastructures intended to contain highly radioactive materials for up to 100,000 years. While their design is driven primarily by technical and regulatory expertise, these facilities also rely on forms of public oversight, consultation, and democratic legitimacy.

The project investigates the capacity of civil society groups to inform, challenge, and oversee repository planning, focusing on how non-expert actors engage with highly specialised knowledge about nuclear technology, geology, and long-term risk. Through interviews, participatory research, and analysis of consultation processes, the project explores how civil society organisations translate complex technical issues into forms accessible to broader publics and how they contribute to ongoing debates about safety, accountability, and intergenerational responsibility.

By analysing the interactions between experts, regulators, and civil society actors, the project aims to identify ways in which participatory governance can be strengthened in the context of infrastructures that must remain secure over geological timescales.

Implementation of the Key Information File for the Swedish Final Repository

(Swedish Nuclear Fuel and Waste Management Company – SKB, 2023–2024; Total value: 1,200,000 kr)

I was co-applicant, together with Professor Anna Storm, on a project commissioned by the Swedish Nuclear Fuel and Waste Management Company (SKB) focused on the development and implementation of a Key Information File for Sweden’s planned final repository for spent nuclear fuel.

The project addressed a central challenge of nuclear waste management: how information about a repository can be communicated in ways that remain intelligible to future generations. Spent nuclear fuel buried in deep geological repositories will remain hazardous for up to 100,000 years, raising questions about what forms of knowledge should be transmitted into the future, how such information should be structured, and who its intended recipients might be.

The research developed a Key Information File designed to present the repository and its associated risks in a form accessible to non-experts. The document forms part of a broader strategy for long-term knowledge preservation concerning nuclear waste repositories.

In developing the Key Information File, the project adopted a meta-perspective on the societal processes involved in long-term nuclear storage. This included examining the interaction between technical and speculative questions, shifting power relations between institutional actors, competing theories of time and temporality, the role of nuclear semiotics, and the ways in which different actors articulate possible future scenarios and their implications.

The project ran between April 2021 and December 2024, with extended funding provided to support further development of the documentation and communication strategy.

PhD Studentship

(Economic and Social Research Council, 2012–2017; Total value: £75,812)

My doctoral research was funded through a 1+3 PhD studentship awarded by the Economic and Social Research Council to support master’s and doctoral training in human geography at the University of Bristol. My PhD thesis, “An Ethology of Technics: Concepts and Experiments with Technological Affects” (awarded May 2018), examined how technologies participate in shaping forms of subjectivity and affect.

Drawing especially on Gilbert Simondon and Gilles Deleuze, the project explored how technological systems influence the production of experience, perception, and embodied practices. The research engaged with cultural geographies of technology and contributed to broader debates concerning the relationship between technological infrastructures, affect, and the formation of social subjects.
