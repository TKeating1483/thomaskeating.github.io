Cultural Geography · Technology and Social Change

Thomas Keating

Researcher and lecturer working across cultural geography, technology studies, speculative thinking, and the politics of long-duration futures.

My work examines how technologies, infrastructures, and institutions organise social life, and how questions of responsibility, memory, and governance endure across deep time. I am particularly interested in nuclear futures, technological abstraction, and the conceptual tools needed to think social change otherwise.

About

I am an Assistant Professor in Technology and Social Change. My background spans human geography, cultural theory, and interdisciplinary work on technology, temporality, and governance.

Across research and teaching, I focus on how social and technical worlds are made imaginable and governable, especially where infrastructures exceed ordinary political timescales. This includes work on speculative method, techno-genesis, and the communication of risk, care, and accountability over the very long term.

Research

Nuclear futures and deep time governance

Research on final repositories for highly radioactive waste, with attention to stewardship, communication, accountability, and the social imagination of futures extending tens of thousands of years.

Technology and social change

Work on technological logics, infrastructures, abstraction, and nonhuman processes, including ontogenetic approaches to technology in geography and adjacent fields.

Speculative thinking

Research engaging geography, philosophy, and critical theory to expand how empirical inquiry is understood, and to explore alternative ways of conceptualising social transformation.

Current projects include: research on accountability in nuclear waste repositories and work on civil society, scrutiny, and repository governance in Sweden.
